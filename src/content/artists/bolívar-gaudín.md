Born in Salto, Uruguay, in 1932, Gaudín moved to Argentina as a teenager, studying at the Escuela de Artes y Oficios in Buenos Aires and training in drawing and painting at Taller Figari in Salto under José Cziffery. His early career included theatrical set design for the Comedia Nacional and independent theater groups in Uruguay and Argentina. Before moving to Europe, he participated in exhibitions across Uruguay, engaging in street art experiences, café installations, and official salons. As a member of Grupo La Cantera, alongside Nelson Ramos and Pascual Grippoli, he contributed to the region’s abstract art movement.

Gaudín formally joined the Madí movement in 1983, bringing a distinct vision that emphasized the interplay of squares, rectangles, and circles. His compositions followed a rigorous structure, using secondary colors and monochrome tones to differentiate his work from that of Kazimir Malevich’s black-and-white approach or Alexander Rodchenko’s primary color explorations. His paintings and sculptures created dynamic rhythms and spatial illusions through asymmetry and geometric precision, reflecting his ongoing search for the infinite within structured form.

Settling in Paris in 1963, Gaudín expanded his international presence with exhibitions at the Musée d’Art Moderne de Paris (“Sudamericanos en París,” 1966), the Salon d’Automne (1969), the First Havana Biennial (1984), and the Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofía in Madrid (1997). His work remains in significant public and private collections worldwide. Gaudín passed away in Paris in 2017, leaving a lasting impact on geometric abstraction and the Madi movement.
