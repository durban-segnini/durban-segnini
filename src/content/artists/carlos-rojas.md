Born in 1933 in Facatativá, a small town in the plains near Bogotá, Rojas initially studied architecture before dedicating himself to art, holding his first exhibition in Bogotá in 1957. His artistic vision was rooted in a unique and exact manner of seeing, a fusion of design—the capacity to discern the formal properties of objects—and life, regarded as the material of freedom. His paintings guide the viewer with precise geometric principles, while dynamic pigments and fragments of everyday materials such as glass and wood invite a sense of wonder in the mundane.

Rojas’ early work in the late 1950s featured figurative still lifes, which soon evolved into cubist aesthetics and collage techniques, as demonstrated in his series *Papeles Pegados* and *Mujeres en faja*. In the 1960s, he embraced extreme rationalism, developing series such as Ingeniería de la Razón and Pueblos, where geometric structure and color interplay highlighted Colombia’s diverse landscapes and cultural fabric. His acclaimed Horizontes series, which received first prize at the 13th São Paulo Biennial (1975), and *Cruzados*, inspired by New York’s urban grid, marked significant milestones in his career.

In the 1970s and beyond, Rojas expanded into sculptural work with constructivist principles, designing public sculptures for parks, university campuses, and urban avenues in Bogotá and Medellín. His work continued to evolve into the 1980s and 1990s with series such as El Dorado, Mater Materia, and Por Pintar, synthesizing his lifelong investigation into geometric abstraction and material presence.
