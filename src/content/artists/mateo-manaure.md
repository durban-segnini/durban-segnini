In Paris, Manaure joined *Los Disidentes*, a collective of Venezuelan artists that rejected academic conservatism and promoted aesthetic reformation (active from 1945-50). Alongside figures like Jesús Rafael Soto and Alejandro Otero, he co-authored the group's radical manifesto No (1950), advocating for geometric abstraction. Returning to Venezuela in 1952, he co-founded the *Taller Libre de Artes* (1948) and organized the *Primera muestra de arte abstracto* (1952) in Caracas. He played a crucial role in Carlos Raúl Villanueva’s architectural project at the *Ciudad Universitaria* in Caracas, contributing major works, including the *Bimural for the Plaza Cubierta* (1954). His geometric phase culminated in an exhibition at the Museo de Bellas Artes (1956) and participation in the Venice Biennale.

During his development period as an artist, his style was highly influenced by expressionism. By the 1950s, he had fully adopted geometric abstraction and continued evolving his visual language, incorporating lyrical abstraction, figuration, and indigenous themes. He also explored audiovisual formats and contributed significantly to graphic design in Venezuela.During the 1960s, Manaure transitioned to lyrical abstraction and figurative painting, producing imaginary landscapes known as *Pinturas Sobremontaje*, which sometimes incorporated photographic collages. He exhibited these at the Sala Mendoza (1962, 1965, 1967). In the late 1960s, he revisited geometric abstraction with his Cuvisiones series, exploring optical effects through color and structure, later exhibited at the Sala Mendoza (1969).  In the 1970s, he taught art and, in 1984, became president of the Asociación Venezolana de Artistas Plásticos. 

He received numerous awards, including the Armando Reverón Award (1994). The Museo de Arte Contemporáneo Mateo Manaure was established in Maturín in 2009 in his honor. In 2012, he created the mural Uracoa in Caracas. His works are held in major collections, including the Galería de Arte Nacional de Caracas, the Museo de Arte Contemporáneo de Caracas, and the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA). He passed away in Caracas in 2018.
